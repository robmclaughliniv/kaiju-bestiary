# Art Bible

## Visual thesis

The Kaiju Bestiary should feel like a recovered premium natural-history archive from a world where ecology became mythic in scale.

The visual language combines:

- Japanese RPG bestiary presentation
- museum field illustration
- expedition photography
- scientific diagrams and marginalia
- weathered Guild bureaucracy
- cinematic environmental scale

## Creature design rules

1. Start with silhouette and ecological function, not decoration.
2. Avoid default humanoid anatomy unless the biology demands it.
3. Every major feature must imply behavior.
4. Use human structures, terrain damage, tracks, and observers to communicate scale.
5. Beauty and horror should coexist.
6. Botanical entries must remain materially distinct from one another.
7. Color should communicate chemistry, habitat, warning, camouflage, or life stage.
8. Creatures should appear to belong to the same world without sharing one body plan.

## Bestiary spread requirements

A finished flagship entry should include:

- one full hero illustration
- one environmental scale scene
- silhouette comparison
- anatomy or life-cycle inset
- regional range map
- field signs and tracks
- Guild threat panel
- handwritten observation or survivor account
- material sample or recovered artifact

## Interface and typography

Use English as the primary language with restrained Japanese characters as graphic accents, classification marks, or section labels. Japanese text must never be used as meaningless visual noise when presented as actual language.

The Guild layer should feel precise, institutional, and slightly oppressive. Local annotations should feel handmade, practical, and lived-in.

## Image-generation prompt pattern

Prompts should specify:

1. organism and ecological role
2. unmistakable silhouette
3. material and anatomical details
4. environment and weather
5. scale cues
6. intended page or image function
7. exclusions that prevent generic output
